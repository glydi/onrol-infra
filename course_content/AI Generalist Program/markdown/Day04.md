## 1. From Words to Images
For the first three days of this program every tool you used worked with words. You typed a question and read back an answer. You learned how a large language model predicts text, and you learned how to shape that text by writing a clear instruction. Today the same underlying ability turns in a new direction. Instead of producing sentences, the AI produces pictures. You describe what you want to see, and the tool draws it.
This is a larger shift than it first appears. Until very recently, making a professional image meant one of two things: you hired a designer, or you spent years learning design software yourself. A shop owner who wanted a festival poster, a job seeker who needed a clean profile picture, a small brand that wanted ten product images for Instagram — all of them had to pay someone or go without. Image generation removes that barrier. A person with no design training can now describe a poster in plain language and have a usable version in under a minute.
That matters for two reasons, and both run through everything you learn today. The first is practical: the work you do every day, whether you run a business, look for a job, or study, often needs visuals, and you can now produce them yourself. The second is commercial: the ability to generate and edit images well is a skill people will pay for. By the end of today you will not only know how to make images, you will understand what that skill is worth and how others are already selling it.
Today focuses on images: generating them, editing them, and enhancing them until they are good enough to use in real work. You will use three tools: Gemini Pro, Google AI Studio, and Canva each for the part of the job it does best. Editing images and sound have their own tools and their own place in the program for now, we give images our full attention.
## 2. How AI Actually Makes an Image
In the same way you do not need to understand a car engine to drive well, you do not need to understand the image generation to generate good images.. But a small amount of understanding about how these tools work will explain a great deal about why they behave the way they do — why the same prompt gives different results each time, why detail in your description matters so much, and why the tool sometimes produces something strange. A few minutes on this topic will save you a lot of confusion later.
The family of tools you are using today is built on something called a diffusion model. The word diffusion refers to spreading out, and it describes the way these models were trained. Imagine taking a clear photo of a box of sweets and slowly covering it in static, like a TV that's lost its signal, until the original image completely disappears. Now imagine teaching a computer to run that process in reverse — starting from nothing but static, and gradually clearing it away until a picture emerges.
That's essentially what's happening every time you generate an image. You type a description, and the model works backwards from noise until it produces something that matches what you asked for

So when you ask one of these tools for an image, it does not paint on a blank canvas the way a person would. It begins with a field of random noise, visual static with no meaning and then refines it in stages, at each stage nudging the noise to look a little more like what your words described. After enough steps, a coherent image emerges out of the static. Your prompt is the instruction that guides every one of those steps. It is the description the model is trying to match as it clears the noise away.
An everyday comparison makes this concrete. Think of a sculptor given a rough block of marble. The statue is not added on; it is revealed by removing everything that is not the statue. The sculptor's idea of the final figure guides which pieces come off. A diffusion model works in a similar spirit: it starts with a shapeless block of noise and removes the parts that do not belong, guided by your description, until the picture you asked for is left standing.
Two practical truths fall straight out of this. The first is why results vary. Because the tool starts from random noise, and the starting noise is different each time, the same prompt produces a different image on each run. This is not a fault. It is why you can generate four versions of a poster and pick the best, and it is a feature you will learn to use deliberately rather than fight against.
The second is why your description carries so much weight. The model only has your words to steer by. Anything you leave unsaid, it fills in from the average of everything it has seen. If you do not say where a scene is set, you will get a generic setting. If you do not say the mood, you will get whatever was most common in its training. This is the same principle you met on Day 3, where a vague text prompt produced a vague answer. The R-T-C-F structure you learned there applies here too, and the next section shows you how.
## 3. Describing an Image So the AI Understands
On Day 3 you learned to structure a text prompt using Role, Task, Context and Format — the R-T-C-F structure. That same discipline is what separates a usable image from a disappointing one. You are still giving the AI a role, a task, the context it needs, and the format you want. What changes is that an image has visual qualities a sentence does not, so you add a few image-specific layers on top of the structure you already know.
There are five things a strong image description almost always specifies. Learn these five and your results improve immediately.
The subject is what the image is of the main thing the eye should land on. Be specific. ‘Shop’ is weak; ‘a small neighbourhood kirana shop with shelves of groceries’ is strong.
Style is the visual treatment of a photograph, a flat illustration, a watercolour, a 3D render, a hand-drawn sketch. The same subject in two different styles is two completely different images, and leaving style unsaid is the most common reason a result feels wrong.
Composition is how the scene is arranged: a close-up or a wide view, the subject centred or off to one side, what sits in the background. This is what makes an image feel designed rather than accidental.
Lighting and mood set the feeling; warm festival lighting, bright daylight, soft evening light. Mood is often the difference between an image that looks cheap and one that looks professional.
Aspect ratio is the shape of the image: a tall rectangle for a phone screen or an Instagram story, a square for a feed post, a wide rectangle for a YouTube thumbnail or a website banner. Asking for the wrong shape means cropping later and losing part of the picture, so decide the shape before you generate.
The way to feel the value of these layers is to watch a weak prompt become a strong one. Suppose a sweet shop owner wants a Diwali promotion image. A first attempt might be only the subject:
A sweet shop for Diwali.
This will produce something, but it could be anything, any style, any shape, any mood — and most versions will be unusable. Now add the five layers, keeping it plain language a non-designer would actually write:
A warm, inviting photograph of a traditional Indian sweet shop counter during Diwali, with neatly arranged trays of laddoo, jalebi and kaju katli in the foreground. Soft golden lighting and a few lit diyas and marigold flowers in the background. Festive and premium feel. Vertical image suited to an Instagram story.
The second prompt names the subject precisely, fixes the style as a photograph, arranges the composition with sweets in front and decoration behind, sets a warm festive mood, and chooses a vertical shape for the platform it is meant for. Nothing in it is technical. It is simply a complete description instead of a partial one, and that completeness is what gives the model enough to work with.
One habit is worth building from the start: describe what you want, not what you don't want. Models respond far better to a clear positive description compared to a list of things to avoid. ‘A clean, uncluttered background’ works better than ‘no mess in the background.’ Say what should be there, and the unwanted things tend to take care of themselves.
## 4. Generating, Controlling and Iterating Images
With a strong description ready, you generate the image. Two of your three tools handle generation, and they suit slightly different moments. Gemini Pro generates images directly inside an ordinary conversation, which makes it the natural choice when you want to describe an image, examine at it, and refine it by simply talking back to the tool. Google AI Studio gives you a more controlled workspace where you can adjust settings and run prompts more deliberately, which makes it useful when you want to test variations of a prompt carefully. For most everyday work you will reach for whichever you have open; the skill you are building transfers between them, because the description is what matters, not the tool you're using.
Generating once is the beginner's mistake. The single most important shift in working with these tools is to treat the first image as a draft, not a verdict. Because the model starts from random noise, you should always generate several versions and choose, rather than accepting the first thing you see. When a result is close but not right, you do not start over — you adjust the description and run it again. This loop of generate, look, adjust, regenerate is the real work, and it is where good results come from.
Adjusting well means changing one thing at a time. If an image is the right scene but the wrong mood, change only the lighting words and keep everything else. If the composition is wrong but the subject is right, change only the composition words. Changing five things at once tells you nothing about which change helped. This is the same iterative refinement you practised with text on Day 3, now applied to pictures.
Beyond simple regeneration, there is a level of control that turns these tools from a novelty into something you can rely on for real work. The most valuable form of control is consistency: getting the same product, character or look to appear across several images rather than a different one each time. A small brand selling a particular packet of chips does not want ten different-looking packets across its posts; it wants its packet, recognisable every time. You achieve this by holding the describing details constant — the same product described the same way — and changing only the scene around it, and, as the next section shows, by feeding the tool a real picture of the actual product to work from.
A worked example shows the loop in motion. Suppose a real-estate agent wants a clean image of a flat for a listing and the first result is dull and badly lit. The agent does not abandon it; the agent adjusts one layer at a time.
Run 1 (prompt): A photograph of a furnished 2BHK flat living room.
Result: Correct room, but flat grey lighting and a cramped angle.
Run 2 (adjust lighting only): A photograph of a furnished 2BHK flat living room, bright natural daylight from a large window, warm and welcoming.
Result: Much better light, but still a tight, awkward angle.
Run 3 (adjust composition only): … same as above, wide-angle view showing the full living room from one corner, tidy and spacious.
Result: Bright, open, listing-ready image.
Notice that each run changed only one layer, so the agent always knew which adjustment did the work. This is the difference between guessing and controlling.
It is here, the moment you can generate a convincing image of almost anything. Because these tools can place a real person, a real brand, or a real logo into any scene you describe, they can also be used to show people and companies doing things they never did. Generating an image of a recognisable individual without their consent, or putting a well-known brand's logo on a product it never made, is not a clever shortcut — it misleads the people who see it and can cause real harm to the person or business depicted. The working rule is simple and you should adopt it now: generate freely from imagination, but when a real, identifiable person or brand is involved, you need their permission, and you should never present a generated image as a real photograph of something that did not happen.
## 5. Working From Your Own Images, Editing and Enhancing
Everything so far has started from a blank description. The most valuable skill in this entire day, especially for anyone doing real business work, is the opposite: starting from an image you already have. These tools do not only generate from nothing — they can take a picture you give them and change it. For a small business this is transformative, because the business already has the real thing. It has a photo of its actual saree, its actual sweet box, its actual shop. It does not want an invented version; it wants its own product made to look professional.
When you give the tool an existing image and describe a change, it edits that image rather than inventing a new one. You can ask it in plain language to do the things that once needed a trained designer. There are four edits you will use constantly, and naming them helps you think clearly about what you are asking for.
Removing takes something out — a stray object in the background of a product photo, a distracting sign behind a person, clutter on a shop counter. You state what needs to be removed, and the tool fills the space behind it naturally.
Replacing swaps one thing for another — changing a plain background behind a product to a clean studio backdrop, or putting a different colour on a wall.
Extending adds to the edges of an image — turning a square photo into a wide one by generating more of the scene on either side, which is how you reshape an image for a platform that needs a different aspect ratio without cropping out the subject.
Cleaning up and enhancing improves the image as it is — sharpening a slightly blurry photo, brightening dull lighting, making colours truer, removing the small flaws that mark an image as amateur.
A grounded example shows how much this is worth. A saree seller photographs a saree on a hanger against a cluttered home wall, with a fan and a switchboard visible behind it. The photo is honest but it looks unprofessional, and a cluttered photo sells less. Rather than rephotographing in a studio, the seller gives AI the existing photo and asks, in plain words, to remove the fan and switchboard and replace the messy wall with a clean, softly lit studio background, keeping the saree exactly as it is. The product is real and unchanged; only its surroundings are improved. The result is a listing image that looks like it came from a professional shoot, but was made from a phone photo in a few minutes.
That last point — keeping the saree exactly as it is — matters beyond appearance. When you edit a product image, the product itself must stay truthful. Improving the lighting on a sweet box is fair; Making the box look fuller than what the customer will actually receive is not.. The line is between making a real product look its best and making it look like something it is not, and a buyer who feels misled does not come back. Keeping edits honest is simply good business as much as it is the right thing to do.
Once an image is generated and edited, it usually needs to be finished into something you actually use — a poster with text on it, a social post with your shop name, a listing with a price. This is where Canva does the job the other AItools don't. Canva is a design tool built for non-designers, and its role in your workflow is assembly: you bring your finished image into Canva, place it into a layout, add your text, your logo and your colours, and export the result at the right size for wherever it is going. The first two AI tools make the picture; Canva turns the picture into a finished piece.
This division of labour raises a question you will face constantly, and it is worth pausing on, because the specific tools in front of you today will not be the ones you use in two years. Tools in this field change faster than almost any other. So the durable skill is not memorising which button sits where in Canva or Gemini; it is learning to ask the right questions when you face a new image job. When something needs doing, ask yourself: what kind of task is this really — generating, editing, or assembling? Which of the tools I know is built for that kind of task? And if none of them fits, how would I find one that does, and judge whether it is any good? A person who thinks this way stays capable no matter how the tools change.
## 6. Making Images That Look Professional
There is a clear line between an image that looks AI-made and one that looks professional, and the difference is rarely the idea. It is the small flaws. Learning to see these flaws is what lets you catch them before anyone else does, and fix them before an image goes out with your name on it.
A handful of giveaways appear again and again. Hands are the most famous — AI tools have long struggled with fingers, and an extra or bent finger is an instant signal that an image is generated, so check hands whenever people appear. Text inside the image is the second: when you ask the tool to put words on a signboard or a packet, it often produces letters that look right at a glance but are misspelled or nonsensical up close, which is exactly why serious text is added afterwards in Canva rather than generated into the picture. Logos and brand marks tend to come out distorted, which looks unprofessional or misrepresents the brand. Lighting is another common problem — if the subject and the background are lit from different directions, the image feels wrong even if the viewer can't quite put their finger on why.
Knowing this, tells you what to do. Check hands, faces and any fine detail before using an image. Do not trust generated text inside an image; add real text yourself in Canva, where you control the spelling. Keep real logos out of the generation step and place the genuine logo in afterwards. And when lighting looks inconsistent, regenerate or fix it rather than hoping no one notices, because people notice without knowing they have.
Beyond the visible flaws sits a practical layer that non-technical users get caught by constantly, and a few plain facts will save you real trouble. Resolution is the first: an image that looks fine on a phone screen can look blurry and pixelated when printed on a banner, because print needs far more detail than a screen. If something is going to be printed, generate and keep it at the largest size the tool offers, and never enlarge a small image to fit a big space, because enlarging only spreads the existing detail thinner. File format is the second: a PNG keeps sharp edges and is right for logos and graphics with text, while a JPG makes a smaller file suited to photographs; for anything going on the web, smaller files load faster, which matters more than people expect.
The third and most easily overlooked layer is the right to use an image commercially. There are two parts to it. The first is the free tiers of these tools: a free plan often limits how many images you can make, may stamp a watermark on what you produce, and sometimes places limits on using the output for business. Before you build a paying service on a tool, read what its free plan actually permits, because running out mid-job or discovering a watermark on a client's poster is a painful way to learn. The second part concerns whose likeness and whose creations you generate. Producing an image in the style of a living, named artist, or generating a recognisable copyrighted character to sell, steps onto ground that is both legally uncertain and ethically questionable, and it is the kind of shortcut that can cost a small business its reputation. Generate from your own ideas and your own products, get permission when a real person is involved, and stay on the safe ground.
## 7. Turning Image Skills Into Income
The skills in this day have direct commercial value, and that value is not theoretical — people across India are already being paid for exactly this work. Businesses everywhere need visuals constantly and most cannot afford a full-time designer or an agency. A person who can produce good images quickly fills a real gap at a price small businesses can actually pay. This section lays out what that work looks like, who buys it, what it is worth, and how you would package your first offering. It is meant to make the opportunity concrete, not to be admired from a distance.
Start with what people actually sell, because the opportunities are specific, not vague. The most common are these. Social media creative packs a set of ready-to-post images for a business's Instagram or WhatsApp, refreshed each month are steady, repeatable work because a business needs new content continuously.Product photography and mockups, taking a seller's plain product photos and turning them into clean, professional listing images, serve the enormous number of small sellers on Instagram, WhatsApp and online marketplaces. Festivals and offers are seasonal but reliable, because every Diwali, Holi, Eid, Pongal and Raksha Bandhan sends shops scrambling for promotional images. Wedding and invitation cards are a large, distinctly Indian market where digital invites are now normal and people will pay for something well designed. And thumbnails and channel art for the growing number of creators and small YouTubers are constant work, because a creator posting weekly needs a new thumbnail every week.
Knowing who buys matters as much as knowing what to sell, because it tells you where to look. The buyers are not large corporations with agencies; they are the businesses around you. The kirana store and the local restaurant that want festivals and weekly social posts. The home-based seller of clothes, jewellery or food who needs product images for an Instagram page. The local real-estate agent who needs listing photos to look better. The wedding planner or the family that wants invitation cards. The small coaching centre that wants posters for a new batch. Looking at the businesses in your own neighbourhood through this lens is the fastest way to find your first customer, because you already know them and they already need this.
Pricing is where beginners hesitate most, so it helps to have rough Indian market ranges in mind, understanding that they vary by city, quality and relationship. A single social media creative might run from a few hundred rupees for simple work to more for polished design. A monthly pack of a dozen or more posts is commonly sold as a retainer running from a few thousand rupees upward, and retainers are the prize because they are predictable income rather than one-off jobs. Product image editing is often priced per image, with discounts for volume. A set of festival creatives is usually a small package price. The pattern to absorb is that ongoing, repeatable arrangements are worth far more over time than single pieces, so once you have a happy customer, the goal is to turn a one-time job into a monthly one.
How you package the first offering decides whether anyone says yes. A vague ‘I make images’ wins no one. A specific, bounded offer does: for example, ‘ten ready-to-post social media images a month for your shop, in your colours, delivered every week, for a fixed monthly price.’ That is concrete, the buyer can picture exactly what they get, and the price is clear. The reliable way to begin is to choose one service from the list above, produce two or three strong samples for a real local business even before they ask using everything in this day, from a strong description to honest editing to a clean finish in Canva and show the business what its own posts could look like. A sample they can see beats any description of what you could do.
For example A home baker wants images to start selling cakes on Instagram and has only ordinary phone photos. You begin from her real photo of a real cake, so the product is truthful. You edit it: remove the cluttered kitchen background, replace it with a clean soft-lit surface, and enhance the lighting so the cake looks its best while remaining the cake she actually sells. You check the result for no warped text, natural lighting, nothing misleading. You bring the finished image into Canva, place it in a square layout, add her business name and a simple price in real, correctly spelled text, and export it at the right size for an Instagram post. The output is a professional, honest, ready-to-post image made from a phone photo and the same process, repeated weekly, is precisely the monthly creative pack you would sell her. That single thread, from description to generation to honest editing to a finished, sellable asset, is the whole of today's skill in one motion.
## 8. Practice
Work through these tasks on your own using the LMS resources and the three tools from today. Each one builds a different part of the skill, so do all four rather than only the one that feels easiest.
Task 1 — Generate and control. Using Gemini Pro or Google AI Studio, write a full image description for a festival promotion for any small business you can imagine, including all five layers: subject, style, composition, lighting and mood, and aspect ratio. Generate it, then improve it across at least three runs, changing only one layer each time. A good result is a clear, festive, platform-shaped image plus a note on what each single change improved.
Task 2 — Edit from a real image. Take a real photo you already have — a product, an object, or a plain room — and use the tools to remove something distracting, replace or clean up the background, and enhance the lighting, while keeping the real subject truthful and unchanged. A good result is a noticeably more professional version of your own photo that still honestly shows the same thing.
Task 3 — Finish a complete creative. Carry one image all the way through: describe and generate (or edit from your own photo), check it for the AI tells covered today, then bring it into Canva, add real text and a name or logo, and export it at the correct size for a specific platform. A good result is a single finished, ready-to-use piece with correctly spelled text and no visible flaws.
Task 4 — Package one sellable service. Choose one image service from today’s list, write it as a specific, bounded offer with a clear deliverable and a rough price, and produce two sample images for a real local business of your choice. A good result is a one-line offer a buyer could immediately understand, backed by two samples that show what they would actually receive.
